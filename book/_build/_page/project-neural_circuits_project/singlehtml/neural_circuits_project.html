NSCI0011 Coursework - Neural Circuits¶

Background¶

A motif is a subgraph with a particular topological structure; for example, there are 13 possible motifs consisting of 3 nodes (see Fig. 1). The aim of motif analysis is to determine which specific motifs are present in a network with a frequency that is significantly different to chance. This can be achieved by repeated sampling from a network in order to construct a motif frequency spectrum (see Fig. 2). Motif analysis has revealed functionally important features in the nervous systems of the macaque, cat and C. elegans [2].

Fig. 1 There are 13 distinct three-node motifs in directed networks. Reprinted from [1]¶

Fig. 2 The frequency spectrum of the 13 3-node motifs in four different brain networks. Reprinted from [1]¶

A ‘connectome’ is a complete description of the structural connectivity of an organism’s nervous system, typically as a graph of nodes (neurons) and directed edges (synaptic connections between neurons). To date, the only whole-animal connectome to be completely determined is the nervous system of hermaphrodite C. elegans, comprising a graph of 460 nodes and 4887 directed edges. In a recent study the connectome of a large part of the Drosophila Melanogaster nervous system was published, consisting of around 25,000 neurons and 20 million synaptic connections (see Fig. 3) [3].

Fig. 3 The connectome of the highlighted portion of the drosophila central brain consists of around 25,000 neurons and 20 million synaptic connections. Reprinted from [3]¶

Project Brief¶

You should perform a motif analysis of the connectome of Drosophila Melanogaster. By reviewing relevant scientific literature, investigate how motif analysis can be performed in nervous systems, then apply appropriate techniques to the Drosophila Melanogaster connectome detailed in [3]. Using diagrams and equations, clearly describe how the motif analysis is performed.
